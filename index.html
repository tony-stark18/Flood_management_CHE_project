Flood

A Flood is an overflow of water that covers land that is normally dry, and can be caused by natural or human factors. It can cause damage to property, infrastructure, and crops, disrupt transportation and communication systems, and result in loss of life.

Causes

Heavy Rainfall

Heavy rainfall is a common cause of floods, especially in areas with poor drainage systems or when rain falls in a short period. The ground can become saturated, and the water cannot be absorbed quickly enough, causing rivers and streams to overflow their banks. The severity of the flood depends on the intensity and duration of the rain, topography of the area, and the drainage capacity of the soil.

Melting snow

During the spring thaw or when there is a sudden increase in temperature, snow on the ground can melt rapidly, causing rivers and streams to swell and overflow their banks. The severity of the flood depends on the amount of snow, the rate of melting, and the capacity of the water bodies to absorb the water.

Dam failure

A dam failure can occur due to poor maintenance, structural damage, or extreme weather conditions. If a dam fails, it can result in a sudden release of a large amount of water, causing flooding downstream. The severity of the flood depends on the size of the dam, the amount of water released, and the location of the downstream communities.

Coastal storms

Coastal storms, such as hurricanes or cyclones, can cause coastal flooding, particularly in low-lying areas. Strong winds and high tides can cause storm surges, which can result in significant flooding. The severity of the flood depends on the intensity and duration of the storm, the topography of the coast, and the protective measures in place.

Deforestation

Cutting down trees and other vegetation reduces the ability of the land to absorb water, leading to soil erosion and an increased risk of flooding. The severity of the flood depends on the extent of deforestation, the topography of the area, and the level of rainfall.

Climate change

Climate change can lead to more extreme weather events, including heavy rainfall and storms, which can cause flooding. Rising sea levels can also result in coastal flooding. The severity of the flood depends on the extent of climate change, the level of greenhouse gas emissions, and the protective measures in place.

Effects

The primary effects of flooding include loss of life and damage to buildings and other structures, including bridges, sewerage systems, roadways, and canals.

Floods also frequently damage power transmission and sometimes power generation, which then has knock-on effects caused by the loss of power. This includes loss of drinking water treatment and water supply, which may result in loss of drinking water or severe water contamination. It may also cause the loss of sewage disposal facilities. Lack of clean water combined with human sewage in the flood waters raises the risk of waterborne diseases, which can include typhoid, giardia, cryptosporidium, cholera and many other diseases depending upon the location of the flood.

Damage to roads and transport infrastructure may make it difficult to mobilize aid to those affected or to provide emergency health treatment. Flood waters typically inundate farm land, making the land unworkable and preventing crops from being planted or harvested, which can lead to shortages of food both for humans and farm animals. Entire harvests for a country can be lost in extreme flood circumstances. Some tree species may not survive prolonged flooding of their root systems.
